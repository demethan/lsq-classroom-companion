# CCJL Partnership Hypothesis

## Context

The Consortium Centre Jules-Léger (CCJL) LSQ math/science lexiques appear to be the strongest fit for the first classroom MVP because they are:

- education-specific;
- science/math aligned;
- LSQ/French;
- validated by CCJL;
- already organized by school-relevant vocabulary.

Visible website terms do not provide an open reuse or AI-training license. Treat the lexiques as reference-only until CCJL provides written permission.

## User-proposed partnership path

Luc believes CCJL may be willing to provide access to its LSQ resources at no cost if the completed tool is shared with CCJL after completion.

This is plausible and worth pursuing, but it must be structured carefully.

## Recommended offer framing

Offer CCJL a partnership rather than a vague content request:

1. We are building a classroom accessibility tool for Deaf LSQ students.
2. CCJL resources are highly aligned with the first MVP vocabulary needs.
3. We would like permission to use a defined subset of CCJL LSQ lexique resources during development/pilot.
4. In return, CCJL would receive access to the completed tool or pilot deployment once usable.
5. CCJL may also be invited to provide validation feedback so the tool respects LSQ and classroom needs.

## Permissions still required in writing

Even if the resources are provided free of charge, written permission should specify:

- which CCJL resources are covered;
- whether videos may be copied/downloaded;
- whether videos may be stored on our server;
- whether videos may be displayed in classroom pilots;
- whether metadata/annotations may be created;
- whether the content may be used for evaluation;
- whether AI training or fine-tuning is allowed, prohibited, or requires later approval;
- whether derivative outputs are allowed;
- required attribution;
- whether CCJL can revoke permission and how removal would work;
- whether the agreement is limited to pilot use or allows broader deployment.

## Important distinction

Access to the completed tool is not the same as a content license.

The project should not assume that “we share the tool” automatically grants rights to copy, host, train on, or redistribute CCJL videos. The exchange should be explicit: tool access in return for defined resource permissions.

## Recommended fallback if CCJL cannot grant broad rights

Ask for a narrow pilot license:

- limited set of 30-100 science/math terms;
- limited to one or a few classroom pilots;
- no public redistribution;
- no AI training unless separately approved;
- internal caching/display only;
- attribution to CCJL;
- deletion on request.

This would still allow a useful MVP without requiring a full dataset license.

## Negotiation posture

Be respectful but precise.

The ask should not sound like “we want your videos for AI.” It should sound like:

- “We want to support Deaf LSQ students in classrooms.”
- “Your validated lexiques are the appropriate authoritative resource.”
- “We want to avoid misuse and obtain written permission.”
- “We are prepared to share the completed tool/pilot benefits with CCJL.”

That is the clean path. Anything looser invites misunderstandings later, which are expensive and usually avoidable.
